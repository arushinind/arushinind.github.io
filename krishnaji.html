The Song of the Soul

The Bhagavad Gita is Krishna's timeless dialogue with Arjuna, a profound guide to life, duty, and liberation. Explore its core wisdom, one verse at a time.

Chapter 2, Verse 47

"You have a right to perform your prescribed duties, but you are not entitled to the fruits of your actions."

This central teaching emphasizes 'Nishkama Karma'—selfless action. Focus on the integrity and dedication of your work, not on the potential rewards or failures. This detaches you from attachment and brings inner peace.

Divine Leela

The 'Leelas' are Krishna's divine plays—stories of his life that are filled with joy, wisdom, and profound symbolism. Click on a story to unfold its magic.

Lifting Govardhan

To protect the people of Vrindavan from the torrential rain sent by a wrathful Lord Indra, Krishna, as a young boy, lifted the entire Govardhan Hill on his little finger, holding it like an umbrella for seven days. This Leela teaches that true refuge is found in the divine, not in demigods or material powers.

Rasa Leela

The 'dance of divine love' where Krishna multiplied himself to dance with each Gopi (cowherd maiden) simultaneously. This symbolizes the soul's intimate, personal relationship with the divine. Each soul feels that Krishna is theirs alone, representing the highest peak of selfless, ecstatic love (prema).

Kaliya's Taming

Krishna fearlessly subdued the venomous serpent Kaliya, who was poisoning the Yamuna river. He danced on the serpent's many hoods, a symbol of conquering the ego and purifying the mind. He didn't kill Kaliya, but banished him, teaching compassion even to one's enemies.

Stealing Butter

As a child, Krishna (known as 'Makhan Chor' or butter thief) would steal butter from the homes of the Gopis. This playful act symbolizes the divine "stealing" the hearts of his devotees—the "butter" representing the purest, most refined essence of their love.

Sacred Symbols

Krishna's form is adorned with symbols, each holding a deep spiritual meaning. Here, they are represented abstractly, as pure concepts of devotion.

The Peacock Feather

A symbol of purity, beauty, and the cosmos. Its all-seeing "eye" represents divine wisdom and the fact that all colors (all beings) are contained within the one divine light.

The Flute (Bansuri)

It represents the human heart, made hollow (free of ego) so that the divine breath can flow through it, creating the celestial music of love that calls all souls back to him.

The Lotus

Growing from mud but remaining pure and untouched, the lotus symbolizes spiritual perfection, detachment, and the beauty that blossoms in the heart of a devotee.
