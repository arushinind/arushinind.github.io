The Lord of the South: Immadi Pulakeshi

"The Emperor who drew a line in the sand that the North could not cross."

By Arush Shetty

History Desk • Astra Intelligence

In the annals of Indian history, few names resonate with the martial brilliance of Immadi Pulakeshi (Pulakeshin II). Ruling from the rock-cut capital of Vatapi (modern-day Badami) between 610 and 642 CE, he was the zenith of the Chalukya dynasty.

While emperors rose and fell, Pulakeshi is immortalized for a single, civilization-defining moment: he was the immovable wall against which the unstoppable force of the North, Emperor Harshavardhana, finally broke.

"Harsha (Joy) was no longer Harsha." — Aihole Inscription, Ravikirti

1. The Rise of the Chalukya Tiger

Pulakeshin II did not inherit a peaceful kingdom. His path to the throne was forged in civil war against his uncle, Mangalesha, who sought to deny him his birthright. After securing his capital, Pulakeshin embarked on a series of military campaigns that expanded the Chalukyan empire from the Arabian Sea to the Bay of Bengal.

He subjugated the Kadambas of Banavasi, the Gangas of Talakad, and forced the Pallava king Mahendravarman I to retreat behind the walls of Kanchipuram. By 615 CE, he was the undisputed master of the Deccan.

2. The Great Clash: Battle of Narmada

While Pulakeshin was consolidating the South, Harshavardhana of Kannauj had conquered the entire North. Harsha was the Sakalauttarapathanatha (Lord of the Entire North). His ambition naturally turned southward, aiming to unite the entire subcontinent under one umbrella.

Around 618-619 CE, Harsha marched with a massive army—historians describe a force teeming with cavalry and infantry—toward the Deccan. The two titans met on the banks of the river Narmada.

Tactical Brilliance

Pulakeshin knew he could not match Harsha’s sheer numbers in open combat. Instead, he used the terrain to his advantage.

  • The Elephant Corps: Pulakeshin’s army guarded the fords of the Narmada with a formidable elephant corps. These beasts were intoxicated with wine before battle, making them fearless and terrifying to the enemy cavalry.
  • Defensive Attrition: The Chalukyan army prevented Harsha’s forces from crossing the river, turning the Narmada into a natural moat.
  • Psychological Warfare: Pulakeshin’s spies and the formidable reputation of his feudatories demoralized the Northern army, which was exhausted from long marches.

The result was decisive. Harsha, who had never known defeat, was forced to retreat. The Aihole inscription poignantly notes that the "joy" (Harsha) of the Northern emperor melted away in fear.

3. Consequences: The North-South Divide

The Battle of Narmada was geopolitically significant. It established the Narmada River as the de facto border between the Northern Empire of Harsha and the Southern Empire of the Chalukyas.

Following this victory, Pulakeshin II assumed the title Parameshwara (Supreme Lord) and Dakshinapathapati (Lord of the South). For the first time, a Southern power was recognized as an equal, if not superior, rival to the dominant Northern power.

International Legacy

Pulakeshin II was not just a regional warlord; he was a global player. Persian historian Al-Tabari records that Pulakeshin exchanged embassies with the Persian Emperor Khusrau II. A painting in the Ajanta Caves (Cave 1) is believed to depict the Persian delegation presenting credentials to Pulakeshin.

4. The Legacy of Badami

Though his life ended in tragedy during a later war with the Pallavas, Immadi Pulakeshi established the template for the Deccan empires that followed—the Rashtrakutas and the Vijayanagara Empire. He proved that the Vindhyas were not just a geographical barrier, but a political one, guarded by the tigers of the South.

His exploits are etched in stone at the Meguti Jain Temple in Aihole, composed by his court poet Ravikirti, ensuring that as long as the stone stands, the name of the Emperor who stopped the North will remain.
